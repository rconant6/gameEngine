0. Color Theory 101 (The Fundamentals)

Before diving into the system design, let's establish the foundational concepts. You don't need to be an artist to understand these - they're just the vocabulary and mental models we'll use throughout.

How Computers Store Color: RGB

RGB = Red, Green, Blue

Every color on your screen is made by mixing three lights:

  • Red (0-255): How much red light
  • Green (0-255): How much green light
  • Blue (0-255): How much blue light
RGB(255, 0, 0) = Pure Red
RGB(0, 255, 0) = Pure Green
RGB(0, 0, 255) = Pure Blue
RGB(255, 255, 0) = Red + Green = Yellow
RGB(255, 255, 255) = All lights on = White
RGB(0, 0, 0) = All lights off = Black

The Problem: RGB is great for computers but terrible for humans. "I want a darker blue" doesn't translate easily to "decrease... which numbers?"

How Humans Think About Color: HSV

HSV = Hue, Saturation, Value

HSV describes color the way artists actually think about it:

Hue (0-360°): "What color is it?"

Imagine a rainbow bent into a circle (the "color wheel"). Hue is the angle on that wheel:

  • 0° = Red
  • 60° = Yellow
  • 120° = Green
  • 180° = Cyan
  • 240° = Blue
  • 300° = Magenta
  • 360° = Back to Red (it's a circle!)

Saturation (0-100%): "How vivid is it?"

Think of it as "how much color vs gray":

  • 0% = Pure gray (no color at all)
  • 50% = Muted, pastel-ish
  • 100% = Maximum intensity, vivid

Red at 0%, 50%, and 100% saturation

Value/Brightness (0-100%): "How light or dark?"

  • 0% = Black (no light)
  • 50% = Medium brightness
  • 100% = Maximum brightness

Red at 0%, 50%, and 100% value

Why HSV matters: When a user says "make this darker," you just decrease V. "Make it more pastel" = decrease S. "Shift toward blue" = adjust H. Much more intuitive!

The Color Wheel

Color Relationships

The color wheel isn't just pretty - it encodes relationships between colors that artists have used for centuries:

Complementary Colors (180° apart)

Colors directly opposite each other on the wheel. They create maximum contrast and make each other "pop."

↔
Red & Cyan
↔
Yellow & Blue

Analogous Colors (neighbors on the wheel)

Colors next to each other (within ~30°). They feel harmonious and cohesive.

Orange → Yellow-Orange → Yellow

Triadic Colors (120° apart)

Three colors evenly spaced around the wheel. Vibrant and balanced.

Red, Green, Blue (the primary triad)

Split-Complementary

A color plus the two colors adjacent to its complement. Less tension than pure complementary.

+
Red + neighbors of Cyan

Warm vs Cool Colors

Temperature

Colors have psychological "temperature":

Warm Colors

Reds, oranges, yellows. Feel energetic, aggressive, advancing (seem closer).

Associated with: fire, sun, energy, danger

Cool Colors

Blues, cyans, some greens. Feel calm, receding (seem farther away).

Associated with: water, sky, ice, calm

Why this matters for pixel art: Shadows are often cooler (shift toward blue). Highlights are often warmer (shift toward yellow). This is called "hue shifting" and it's the #1 technique that makes pixel art look professional.

Value is King

The Most Important Dimension

Professional artists will tell you: If your values (lights and darks) are wrong, no amount of pretty colors will save you. If your values are right, almost any colors will work.

Try this mental exercise: imagine your art in black and white. Can you still read what's going on? Are the important things lighter or darker than the background? That's value doing its job.

Good value range

Flat (bad)

Palette implication: A good palette has colors across multiple value levels. Our system tracks this with the Value dimension (Darkest → Dark → Mid → Light → Lightest).

0.5 Pixel Art & Color (Why This Matters)

Pixel art has unique constraints that make color choices especially important.

Limited Palettes

Every Color Must Earn Its Place

Unlike digital painting where you can use millions of colors, pixel art traditionally uses very limited palettes:

  • Game Boy: 4 colors (shades of green)
  • NES: 25 colors on screen at once
  • PICO-8: 16 colors
  • Modern pixel art: Often 16-64 colors by choice

These limits aren't just historical - they're a feature. Constraints force creativity and create visual cohesion. Our 64-color limit follows this tradition.

Color Ramps (The Secret Weapon)

What's a Ramp?

A "ramp" is a sequence of colors that go from shadow to highlight for a single material. For example, a skin ramp might have 4-5 colors from deep shadow to bright highlight.

The Naive Approach (Don't Do This)

Just making a color darker or lighter by adjusting only value:

Same hue, same saturation, only value changes. Looks flat and "digital."

Hue Shifting (The Pro Technique)

Shift the hue as you change value: shadows go toward blue/purple, highlights go toward yellow/orange:

Shadows shift toward purple, highlights toward yellow. Looks "alive."

Why does this work?

  • In real life, shadows often pick up ambient blue from the sky
  • Highlights often pick up warm yellow from sunlight
  • It mimics how traditional painters use complementary colors in shadows
  • It creates more visual interest than just "darker same color"

This is why our ramp generator exists: It automatically applies hue shifting to create professional-looking ramps from a single base color.

Why Hardware Palettes Matter

NES, PICO-8, Game Boy, etc.

Old game consoles had fixed or limited color palettes due to hardware constraints. These palettes have become iconic:

Game Boy (DMG)

4 shades of green - that's it! Yet iconic games were made with just these.

PICO-8

16 carefully chosen colors. Modern "fantasy console" with intentional limitations.

Why we include these:

  • Nostalgia/Aesthetic: Many artists want that retro look
  • Proven palettes: These colors were chosen by skilled engineers/artists to work well together
  • Constraints breed creativity: Limiting yourself to NES colors forces interesting choices
  • Learning tool: Study how masters used limited palettes

Palette Harmony

Making Colors Work Together

A random grab-bag of colors usually looks bad. Harmonious palettes share something in common:

Shared Undertone

All colors in the palette have a slight tint of the same hue. For example, everything slightly warm (pushed toward yellow/orange).

Limited Hue Range

Instead of using the whole color wheel, limit yourself to a section of it (analogous colors) plus maybe one accent.

Consistent Saturation

If some colors are super vivid and others are muted, they fight each other. Pick a saturation "zone" and mostly stick to it.

Value Hierarchy

Make sure you have a good distribution of darks, mids, and lights. Don't cluster everything in the middle.

What our system provides:

  • Preset palettes: Already harmonious, designed by artists
  • Harmony generators: Create complementary/triadic/analogous palettes automatically
  • Family browsing: Colors within a family were designed to work together
  • Analysis tools: (Future) Warn you if your palette has gaps or clashes

2. Color Math Foundation

Everything else depends on these core algorithms. Build these first. This section includes the actual math so you can implement it.

RGB to HSV Conversion

What: Convert RGB (0-255 per channel) to HSV (Hue 0-360, Sat 0-100, Value 0-100)

Why: HSV is how humans perceive color. All bucketing and queries work in HSV space.

Where: Core color math module, used everywhere

The Algorithm (Step by Step)

  1. Normalize RGB to 0-1 range:
    • r = R / 255
    • g = G / 255
    • b = B / 255
  2. Find min and max of r, g, b:
    • max = maximum of (r, g, b)
    • min = minimum of (r, g, b)
    • delta = max - min
  3. Calculate Value (V):
    • V = max (that's it!)
    • Scale to 0-100: V = max * 100
  4. Calculate Saturation (S):
    • If max = 0: S = 0 (black has no saturation)
    • Otherwise: S = (delta / max) * 100
  5. Calculate Hue (H):
    • If delta = 0: H = 0 (gray, hue is undefined)
    • If max = r: H = 60 * ((g - b) / delta) mod 6
    • If max = g: H = 60 * ((b - r) / delta + 2)
    • If max = b: H = 60 * ((r - g) / delta + 4)
    • If H < 0: H = H + 360

Example

RGB(255, 128, 64)
  • Normalized: r=1.0, g=0.502, b=0.251
  • max=1.0, min=0.251, delta=0.749
  • V = 1.0 * 100 = 100%
  • S = (0.749 / 1.0) * 100 = 74.9%
  • H = 60 * ((0.502 - 0.251) / 0.749) = 60 * 0.335 = 20° (orange)

Edge Cases to Handle

  • Pure black (0,0,0): S and H are undefined, return H=0, S=0, V=0
  • Grays (R=G=B): S=0, H is undefined but return 0
  • Hue wrap-around: Make sure negative hue values wrap to positive (add 360)
HSV to RGB Conversion

What: Convert HSV back to RGB

Why: After generating colors in HSV (easier for humans), we need RGB for display

Where: Palette generators, color editor

The Algorithm

  1. Normalize inputs:
    • H in range 0-360
    • S and V in range 0-1 (divide by 100 if percentage)
  2. Calculate chroma and intermediate values:
    • C = V * S (chroma)
    • X = C * (1 - |((H / 60) mod 2) - 1|)
    • m = V - C
  3. Map hue to RGB sector:
    • 0° ≤ H < 60°: (r', g', b') = (C, X, 0)
    • 60° ≤ H < 120°: (r', g', b') = (X, C, 0)
    • 120° ≤ H < 180°: (r', g', b') = (0, C, X)
    • 180° ≤ H < 240°: (r', g', b') = (0, X, C)
    • 240° ≤ H < 300°: (r', g', b') = (X, 0, C)
    • 300° ≤ H < 360°: (r', g', b') = (C, 0, X)
  4. Add m and scale to 0-255:
    • R = (r' + m) * 255
    • G = (g' + m) * 255
    • B = (b' + m) * 255

Perceptual Color Distance

What: Measure how "different" two colors look to human eyes

Why: Used for similarity search, quantization, duplicate detection

Where: Query system, palette analysis, "find similar" feature

Option 1: Simple Weighted Euclidean (Good Enough to Start)

Human eyes are more sensitive to green, less to blue. Weight accordingly:

distance = sqrt(
    2 * (r1 - r2)² +     // Red weight: 2
    4 * (g1 - g2)² +     // Green weight: 4 (most sensitive)
    3 * (b1 - b2)²       // Blue weight: 3
)

Or a slightly better approximation that accounts for the average red value:

avg_r = (r1 + r2) / 2
dr = r1 - r2
dg = g1 - g2
db = b1 - b2

distance = sqrt(
    (2 + avg_r/256) * dr² +
    4 * dg² +
    (2 + (255 - avg_r)/256) * db²
)

Option 2: Distance in HSV Space

Sometimes comparing in HSV makes more sense (e.g., finding colors with similar hue):

// Hue is circular! Use shortest angular distance
hue_diff = min(|h1 - h2|, 360 - |h1 - h2|)

// Normalize to 0-1 for comparison
hue_dist = hue_diff / 180  // max possible is 180

distance = sqrt(
    (hue_dist * weight_h)² +
    ((s1 - s2) / 100 * weight_s)² +
    ((v1 - v2) / 100 * weight_v)²
)

Option 3: OKLAB (Best Perceptual Uniformity)

OKLAB is a modern color space designed for perceptual uniformity. Colors that "look equally different" to humans have equal distances in OKLAB. More complex to implement but gives best results for similarity search.

Recommendation: Start with weighted RGB. It's simple and good enough for most cases. Upgrade to OKLAB later if similarity search results feel "off."

What's a "Good" Threshold?

  • < 10: Colors are very similar, might be duplicates
  • 10-30: Similar colors, good for finding alternatives
  • 30-60: Related colors, same general family
  • > 60: Distinctly different colors

(These are rough guidelines for the weighted RGB formula. Tune based on testing!)

Hue Shifting (The Magic Sauce)

What: Rotate hue while adjusting saturation/value for professional-looking ramps

Why: This is THE technique that makes pixel art look professional vs amateur

Where: Ramp generator, "generate darker/lighter" features

Basic Concept

Instead of just making a color darker (reducing V), we also shift the hue:

  • Shadows: Shift toward blue/purple (add 210-270° or subtract 90-150°)
  • Highlights: Shift toward yellow (add 30-60°)

The Algorithm for Shadow

function createShadow(h, s, v, intensity):
    // intensity: 0.0 = base color, 1.0 = deepest shadow

    // 1. Reduce value (make darker)
    new_v = v * (1.0 - intensity * 0.7)  // Don't go fully black

    // 2. Shift hue toward blue/purple
    hue_shift = -20 * intensity  // negative = toward blue
    new_h = (h + hue_shift) mod 360

    // 3. Optionally adjust saturation
    // Shadows often slightly desaturate
    new_s = s * (1.0 - intensity * 0.1)

    return (new_h, new_s, new_v)

The Algorithm for Highlight

function createHighlight(h, s, v, intensity):
    // intensity: 0.0 = base color, 1.0 = brightest highlight

    // 1. Increase value (make lighter)
    new_v = v + (100 - v) * intensity * 0.8  // Approach but don't hit 100

    // 2. Shift hue toward yellow
    hue_shift = 25 * intensity  // positive = toward yellow
    new_h = (h + hue_shift) mod 360

    // 3. Highlights often desaturate as they approach white
    new_s = s * (1.0 - intensity * 0.3)

    return (new_h, new_s, new_v)

Generating a Full Ramp

function generateRamp(base_h, base_s, base_v, steps):
    ramp = []
    mid = steps / 2

    for i in 0..steps:
        if i < mid:
            // Shadow range
            intensity = (mid - i) / mid  // 1.0 at darkest, 0.0 at base
            color = createShadow(base_h, base_s, base_v, intensity)
        else if i > mid:
            // Highlight range
            intensity = (i - mid) / (steps - 1 - mid)
            color = createHighlight(base_h, base_s, base_v, intensity)
        else:
            // Base color
            color = (base_h, base_s, base_v)

        ramp.push(color)

    return ramp

Color Blending / Interpolation (Lerp)

What: Create intermediate colors between two endpoints

Why: Gradients, smooth ramps, transition colors

Where: Palette generators, gradient tools

Linear Interpolation (Lerp) in RGB

The simplest approach - just average each channel:

function lerpRGB(color1, color2, t):
    // t: 0.0 = color1, 1.0 = color2, 0.5 = midpoint
    r = color1.r + (color2.r - color1.r) * t
    g = color1.g + (color2.g - color1.g) * t
    b = color1.b + (color2.b - color1.b) * t
    return (r, g, b)

Problem: RGB lerp can produce muddy, desaturated midpoints, especially between complementary colors.

Red to Cyan in RGB: the middle is gray/brown. Yuck!

Interpolation in HSV

Better results by interpolating hue, saturation, and value separately:

function lerpHSV(color1, color2, t):
    // Handle hue wrap-around (it's circular!)
    h1 = color1.h
    h2 = color2.h

    // Find shortest path around the circle
    diff = h2 - h1
    if diff > 180: diff = diff - 360
    if diff < -180: diff = diff + 360

    h = (h1 + diff * t) mod 360
    s = color1.s + (color2.s - color1.s) * t
    v = color1.v + (color2.v - color1.v) * t

    return (h, s, v)

Result: Colors stay saturated, transition through the color wheel naturally.

Red to Cyan in HSV: goes through purple/blue. Vibrant!

Which Direction Around the Wheel?

When interpolating hue, you can go clockwise or counter-clockwise. The algorithm above takes the shortest path. Sometimes you want the long way:

// Force long path (for rainbow effects)
if diff > 0: diff = diff - 360  // Go the other way
if diff < 0: diff = diff + 360
Bucket Classification

What: Assign each color to Hue/Value/Saturation/Temperature buckets

Why: Enables fast queries like "give me all dark reds"

Where: Comptime for library, runtime for user colors

Hue Bucket Assignment

function getHueBucket(h, s, v):
    // First check for neutral (gray) - saturation too low to have hue
    if s < 15:
        return NEUTRAL

    // Check for brown (special case of dark/muted orange)
    if h >= 15 and h <= 45 and s >= 20 and s <= 70 and v >= 15 and v <= 60:
        return BROWN

    // Standard hue ranges (approximate, tune as needed)
    if h < 15 or h >= 345:    return RED
    if h < 45:                return ORANGE
    if h < 70:                return YELLOW
    if h < 90:                return LIME
    if h < 150:               return GREEN
    if h < 180:               return TEAL
    if h < 210:               return CYAN
    if h < 260:               return BLUE
    if h < 290:               return INDIGO
    if h < 320:               return PURPLE
    if h < 345:               return MAGENTA/PINK

    return RED  // shouldn't reach here

Value Bucket Assignment

function getValueBucket(v):
    if v < 20:  return DARKEST
    if v < 40:  return DARK
    if v < 60:  return MID
    if v < 80:  return LIGHT
    return LIGHTEST

Saturation Bucket Assignment

function getSaturationBucket(s):
    if s < 15:  return GRAY
    if s < 40:  return MUTED
    if s < 70:  return MODERATE
    return VIVID

Temperature Assignment

function getTemperature(hue_bucket):
    switch hue_bucket:
        case RED, ORANGE, BROWN, YELLOW, PINK:
            return WARM
        case LIME, GREEN, PURPLE, NEUTRAL:
            return NEUTRAL_TEMP
        case TEAL, CYAN, BLUE, INDIGO:
            return COOL
        case MAGENTA:
            // Magenta is tricky - check if more red or blue
            if original_h < 315:
                return COOL   // more blue
            else:
                return WARM   // more red
Harmony Generation

What: Generate harmonious color palettes from a base color

Why: Helps users create cohesive palettes quickly

Where: Harmony picker UI, palette generators

Complementary (180° apart)

function complementary(h, s, v):
    return [(h, s, v), ((h + 180) mod 360, s, v)]

Triadic (120° apart)

function triadic(h, s, v):
    return [
        (h, s, v),
        ((h + 120) mod 360, s, v),
        ((h + 240) mod 360, s, v)
    ]

Analogous (adjacent colors)

function analogous(h, s, v, spread = 30):
    return [
        ((h - spread * 2) mod 360, s, v),
        ((h - spread) mod 360, s, v),
        (h, s, v),
        ((h + spread) mod 360, s, v),
        ((h + spread * 2) mod 360, s, v)
    ]

Split-Complementary

function splitComplementary(h, s, v, spread = 30):
    complement = (h + 180) mod 360
    return [
        (h, s, v),
        ((complement - spread) mod 360, s, v),
        ((complement + spread) mod 360, s, v)
    ]

Tetradic / Square (90° apart)

function tetradic(h, s, v):
    return [
        (h, s, v),
        ((h + 90) mod 360, s, v),
        ((h + 180) mod 360, s, v),
        ((h + 270) mod 360, s, v)
    ]

  • Color indices array (for leaf nodes, or aggregated for branch queries)

Query approach: Traverse to target node, return its color indices. For branch nodes, recursively collect all descendant colors.

Query Execution

Single Dimension Query
"All red colors" -> Direct bucket lookup -> Return hue_red slice
+
Multi-Dimension Query
"Dark warm reds" -> Intersect value_dark + temp_warm + hue_red
Small arrays, intersection is fast
+
Family Query
"All NES colors" -> Tree lookup -> Return family's color indices
"All 8-bit colors" -> Tree traversal -> Aggregate all children

6. Query System

Query Types

By Dimension
  • getByHue(Hue.Red) -> slice of Colors
  • getByValue(Value.Dark) -> slice of Colors
  • getBySaturation(Saturation.Vivid) -> slice of Colors
  • getByTemperature(Temperature.Warm) -> slice of Colors
By Family
  • getByFamily(Family.NES) -> slice of Colors (leaf only)
  • getByCategory(Category.Hardware) -> slice of Colors (all descendants)
Compound Queries (Builder Pattern)

Chain filters to narrow down:

  • query().hue(.Red).value(.Dark).temperature(.Warm).execute()
  • Or struct-based: getFiltered(FilterSpec{ .hue = .Red, .value = .Dark })
Similarity Query
  • findSimilar(color, max_results) -> sorted slice by distance
  • Uses bucket membership as first-pass filter (same hue = likely similar)
  • Then computes perceptual distance for candidates
Relative Queries
  • getDarker(color) -> colors in same hue, lower value bucket
  • getLighter(color) -> colors in same hue, higher value bucket
  • getMoreSaturated(color) -> same hue, higher saturation bucket
  • getLessSaturated(color) -> same hue, lower saturation bucket

Similarity Algorithm

Distance Calculation (On-The-Fly)

Factors to weight:

  1. Hue difference (circular distance on color wheel)
  2. Value difference (linear)
  3. Saturation difference (linear)
  4. Optional: Family affinity bonus (same family = closer)

Optimization: Use bucket membership to filter candidates before computing full distance.

  • Same hue bucket? High chance of similarity - compute distance
  • Adjacent hue buckets? Medium chance - maybe compute
  • Opposite hue buckets? Low chance - skip unless exhaustive search

7. User Color Handling

User Color Entry (Lightweight)

Runtime Data Only
  • RGBA values
  • Display name (optional, user-provided)
  • Source reference (which library color it came from, if any)
  • Modification distance (how far from source)
  • Custom use-case tags (0-4)
  • Locked flag

NOT stored: Bucket memberships (computed on demand using same algorithms as library colors)

Home Bias (Tag Stability)

When User Edits a Color

Computed tags (Hue/Value/Sat/Temp): Always recompute from RGB. No bias. If the color crosses a bucket boundary, the tag changes.

Family tag: Sticky with bias toward "home"

  1. Original family is remembered as "home"
  2. Small edits: Keep home family, mark as "modified"
  3. Calculate distance from nearest color in home family
  4. If distance exceeds threshold: Prompt user or auto-assign "Custom"
  5. User can manually re-home to any family

Use-case tags: Inherited from source, then independent. User edits don't auto-change these.

Visual Feedback for Modified Colors
  • Unmodified library color: Clean swatch
  • Modified (within threshold): Subtle indicator (dot, different border)
  • Modified (beyond threshold): Warning indicator, "Custom" label

8. Comptime vs Runtime Split

100% Comptime (Library Colors)

Built at Compile Time
  • All 1000+ color RGBA values
  • All computed tags (hue, value, sat, temp buckets)
  • All family assignments
  • All use-case tag assignments
  • All bucket index arrays
  • Family tree structure
  • Color names/identifiers

Result: Zero runtime cost for library. Instant queries.

Runtime Only (User/Palette Colors)

Computed at Runtime
  • RGBA values (from user input or tweaking)
  • Computed tags (same bucket algorithms, run on demand)
  • Source reference
  • Modification distance
  • Custom names and tags

Note: 64 colors max in palette. Linear operations are fine.

Performance Reality

Where Speed Actually Matters
Operation Frequency Acceptable Time Actual Time
Painting (palette lookup) Every frame < 1ms O(1), nanoseconds
Color blending Every brush stroke < 1ms Pure math, nanoseconds
Library query User clicks button < 10ms Bucket lookup, < 1ms
Similarity search User clicks button < 50ms ~1000 comparisons, < 5ms
Render canvas Every frame < 16ms (60fps) This is the real bottleneck

Conclusion: Color system is never the bottleneck. Optimize for clarity first.

11. Open Questions

Bucket Boundaries

Exact hue angle ranges for each bucket? Should we derive from analyzing the actual library colors, or use fixed standard ranges?

  • Fixed: Simpler, predictable
  • Derived: Might better match the actual color distribution
Use-Case Tag Assignment

How to assign use-case tags to 1000+ library colors?

  • Manual curation: Most accurate, very time-consuming
  • Auto-suggest from family + name keywords: Faster, might miss nuances
  • Hybrid: Auto-suggest, then manual review/correction
Tree Node Aggregation

Does each branch node store an aggregated color list, or compute it on query by walking children?

  • Pre-aggregated: Faster queries, more memory, comptime cost
  • Compute on query: Slower queries, less memory
  • Recommendation: Pre-aggregate at comptime (memory is cheap, queries should be instant)
Neutral Saturation Threshold

How low must saturation be before a color is classified as "Neutral" regardless of hue?

  • 10%: Conservative, only very gray colors
  • 15%: Middle ground
  • 20%: Aggressive, more colors become Neutral
Brown Detection Formula

Exact conditions for Brown vs Dark Orange?

  • Hue range: 15-45 degrees? 10-50?
  • Saturation range: 20-70%? 15-65%?
  • Value range: 15-60%? 10-55%?
  • May need to tune empirically by testing against actual brown colors in library
Home Bias Threshold

How far can a color drift before losing its family tag?

  • Fixed global threshold (e.g., perceptual distance > 30)?
  • Per-family thresholds (tight families like GameBoy have smaller threshold)?
  • User-configurable preference?
Palette History Depth

How many palette undo steps to keep?

  • 20: Low memory, might not be enough
  • 50: Good balance
  • Unlimited: Memory grows, but palette ops are small

Design Principles to Remember

  • Comptime everything possible - Library is static, use Zig's compile-time features
  • 64 colors is small - Don't over-optimize palette operations
  • Users think in families - "Give me NES colors" is the primary mental model
  • Tags are metadata, not identity - RGB values are the source of truth
  • Bias toward home - Users want edited colors to remember where they came from
  • Separate undo stacks - Palette history is independent of canvas history
